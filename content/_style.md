# Editorial Style — The Supergravity Voice

If you are a session writing lessons, your single job is to sound like
Supergravity, not like Wikipedia, Khan Academy, or a textbook. This guide is
short on purpose. Read it every time.

## The voice

- **Literary, but plain.** The model is a good magazine essayist: The New
  Yorker, The Atlantic's science writing, the best of The Economist. Not
  academic. Not bloggy.
- **Sentences that a smart adult would actually enjoy reading.** Vary length.
  Prefer specifics over abstractions. Cut throat-clearing.
- **An implied point of view.** Not opinionated, not neutral — the voice of
  a reader who has thought about this subject and has favorite passages.
- **Respectful of the reader.** Never condescending, never schoolteacher-ish.
  Assume a curious adult who has not studied this subject but is bright.
- **Allergic to filler.** No "In this lesson, we'll explore…". No "It is
  important to note that…". Start with the subject itself.

## The shape of a lesson

1. **Lead (`lead` block).** One paragraph. Open with a concrete image, a
   specific historical moment, or a surprising fact. Not a definition.
2. **3–5 sections (`h2` + one or two `p`).** Each section teaches one idea
   well. Name the section for the idea, not the topic.
3. **At most one `quote`.** Only use it if the quote is a famous one from a
   primary source (Euclid, Darwin, Feynman, etc.). No motivational filler.
4. **At most one `callout`.** For a surprising aside or a key warning.
5. **At most one `interactive`.** Reference an existing widget by key.
6. **A closing paragraph.** One sentence that resolves the lead and points
   forward — but no meta-text like "In summary..." or "You've learned...".

## Length

- 300–600 words of prose in `body` (excluding the `resources` block).
- Dek (tagline): 10–18 words, newspaper-dek voice.
- Callout or quote text: under 40 words.

## Sample openings — ✅ and ❌

✅ "Before geometry had proofs, before algebra had letters, humans were tying
knots in cords and stacking pebbles."

✅ "In 1831, a young man on a sailing ship wrote in his diary that the
Galápagos finches appeared to be closely related to each other."

❌ "In this lesson, we'll explore the fundamentals of arithmetic and why it's
important in everyday life."

❌ "Biology is the study of living organisms."

## Voice for different categories

- **Math / Sciences:** historical, concrete, confident. Introduce ideas through
  the person who had them. Never apologize for the math.
- **Trades / Crafts:** tactile, practical, proud. Treat the trade as a deep
  subject, not a blue-collar afterthought. Lead with the body of the work.
- **Humanities:** close-read, attentive to language, occasionally wry. Quote
  sparingly but well.
- **Life Skills:** warm, direct, unpatronizing. Assume the reader is an adult
  who has been putting this off.
- **Wisdom Traditions:** reverent, curious, non-partisan. Every tradition gets
  the same care. No proselytizing; no dismissal.

## On resources

- **Quality over quantity.** Five excellent resources beat fifteen mediocre
  ones. The `why` field is the most important — it tells the reader WHY this
  resource and not another.
- **Prefer primary sources** where they exist and are accessible. Euclid's
  *Elements* beats a summary of Euclid. Feynman's actual lectures beat
  someone's summary of them.
- **Video quality bar:** 3Blue1Brown, Numberphile, Crash Course, Veritasium,
  Kurzgesagt, the BBC, PBS, a named lecturer from a real university. Prefer
  creators who have thought about how to teach this, not SEO content farms.
- **Book quality bar:** classic (a book that has been in print for 20+ years),
  standard (the reference people actually cite), or current (a genuinely
  excellent modern entry). Three slots, three kinds.
- **No affiliate links. No sponsored content. No LinkedIn think-pieces.**
- **Kill dead links.** Every URL must be verified with `WebFetch` before it
  ships. If you cannot reach it, leave it out.

## What we don't do

- No markdown bullets inside lesson body. Prose only.
- No emojis.
- No "fun facts" or "did you know?" framing.
- No second-person drill: "You will learn that...". Address the reader only
  in the way a good essay does — sparingly.
- No padding. If a lesson is short and honest, ship it short and honest.

## A sentence to put on the wall

Write each lesson as if it were going into a book that will still be read in
twenty years. Then remove the sentences that won't make it.
